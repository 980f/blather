Command Details

Tack

Swings the bow through the wind.

  1. The fore course might be left set during the maneuver, but often is doused.
  2. The spanker is brought amidships from the lee side which, along with the rudder, turns the bow into the wind.
  3. The headsails are eased with the spanker moving amidships.
  4. The courses are doused while still on the initial tack as the bow approaches the wind.
  5. After the bow passes the eye of the wind the main stack is hauled to the new tack, the fore is still on the initial tack and may slow the ship some but is helping the pivot.
  6. The headsails are backed to keep the rotation going, and the spanker is readied to move to the new tack.
  7. The headsails and staysails are shifted once the ship is definitely past the eye of the wind.
  8. The fore stack is hauled around.

Wear

Swing the stern through the wind.

  1. Spanker and rest of mizzen sails are doused.
  2. The main course is doused and both fore and main stacks are braced square.
  3. The mizzen 'stack' is moved to the new tack, but is still doused.
  4. As the stern points into the wind the fore-n-aft sails are shifted.
  5. Fore-n-aft are shifted, mizzen ones set.
  6. As the new heading is approached the spanker is set.

Box Haul

A wear but first swing the bow into the wind then let the wind push the ship back through the first part of the subsequent wear.

  1. Spanker to midships starts the turn towards the wind.
  2. The fore course is doused,
  3. then the spanker is doused.
  4. The main course is doused and the stack brought to square.
  5. The fore stack is hauled to catch the wind backwards pushing the vessel backwards.
  6. After the stern goes from lee to weather the fore is hauled around and is now filled normally.
  7. The remainder is much the same as a wear.
  8. The fore crew lay on the deck exhausted.

Details on actions

Rise tacks and sheets:
This is the command to douse the course; however, the clews and bunts on the leeward side should be hauled only about a fathom from the yard rather than all the way up to the yard.
Mainsail haul:
On the main, easers let go of the braces (depending on wind conditions) and haulers haul until the yards come up to sharp on the opposite tack.
The person assigned to handle lifts and sheets will cast off the leeward course sheet, cast off the leeward course lift, haul on the opposite course lift until the yards come square, then belay that lift and haul on the now-leeward course sheet (i.e., the one on the opposite side to the sheet that was cast off first) until all slack is out.
Do not belay the sheets.
Let go and haul:
On the fore, easers let go of the braces (depending on wind conditions) and haulers haul until the yards come up to sharp on the opposite tack.
The person assigned to handle lifts and sheets will cast off the leeward course sheet, cast off the leeward course lift, haul on the opposite course lift until the yards come square, then belay that lift and haul on the now-leeward course sheet (i.e., the one on the opposite side to the sheet that was cast off first) until all slack is out.
Do not belay the sheets.
If the forecourse is being carried during the maneuver, one person must stay on the lee sheet to ease it out under control, a second person must handle the lifts as above, and a third Crew Member is needed to haul the sheet on the other side. In other words, you’re treating the sheets like a set of braces. The lee sheet must be belayed after hauling.
On the head deck, one Crew Member needs to ease off the working tack and board the opposite tack as the yards come around.
Hold the braces:
On the fore or the main, pause in heaving around the braces by taking a turn or two around the pin (on both easing/hauling sides) to keep the yards under control while waiting for the next command.
Brace the yards square to the wind:
On the fore or the main, easers ease the leeward braces under control and haulers heave around the opposite side under the direction of the Mast Captain, who will be watching the mast’s flag to ensure that the sails remain perpendicular to the direction of the wind; the yards may need to be held repeatedly during this process.
Brace ashiver:
On the fore or the main, easers ease the leeward braces under control and haulers heave around the opposite side under the direction of the Mast Captain, who will be watching the mast’s flag to ensure that the sails remain parallel to the direction of the wind, thus depowering the sails; the yards may need to be held repeatedly during this process.
Heave around the braces:
On the fore or main, easers ease the leeward braces and haulers heave around the opposite until the yards reach the required point (e.g., 2 points on the new tack).
Pass headsails/staysails/all fore-and-aft sails:
On the fore (if headsails are included in this command), main, and mizzen, ease the previously working sheet until the person on the now-leeward side has control; then let go the lazy sheet and harden in the new working sheet.
Ease/let fly headsails:
On the fore, slack away the leeward sheets until the sails are depowered, i.e., luffing, without letting them flog.
Back headsails:
After the bow has passed through the wind, the head deck crew hauls headsail sheets taut causing them to fill backwinded; do not allow them to fill again on the old tack.
Spanker amidships:
Haul the spanker amidships using the spanker sheet and the preventer. The order of moving the preventer and hauling the spanker boom to midships depends on the maneuver and weather conditions (listen to the Mast Captain’s commands). When moving the spanker boom, haul on the weather spanker sheet until the boom is midship.
Slack away spanker sheet/put the boom to weather:
Ease the newly windward spanker sheet, moving the spanker boom out to the new leeward as far as the Conn wants it; adjust the preventer if necessary.
Set courses:
After the maneuver is completed, at the discretion of the Conn, the main and/or fore will reset the courses on the new tack.
Helm commands:
Helm commands will be given throughout each maneuver on a situational basis. During a tack, the traditional command to start the maneuver is “Helm’s a’lee,” which means to turn the wheel towards weather; however, the actual direction given the helmsman may not be to turn the helm hard over.
